Resilience is a muscle

built through experience. It’s interesting that organizations that experienced a major breach are actually the most confident that they can survive a disruption. Getting hit seems to build resilience more so than fear.

Everyone is building the plane while flying it.

Half of respondents are still developing a digital sovereignty strategy despite many of those people calling it a “high priority.” There is a tension between this sense of urgency and actual readiness.

AI eats everything.

If given incremental budget, 70% would invest in AI first. Sovereignty has to fight for oxygen even among people who say it matters.

Adversaries are also treated like allies.

65% see hyperscalers as the most relevant vendors for supporting sovereign workloads even though their foreign jurisdiction (for most of the world) creates sovereignty concerns in the first place. S

Sovereignty is already (quietly) influencing purchasing.

Only 3% said sovereignty wasn’t a consideration at all even though it isn’t a driving factor yet in decision making.

Open source is practical not ideological.

In this context, open source is less about “democratic ideals” and “reducing dependence on Big Tech” and more about flexibility and innovation. Much of sovereignty narrative frames open source as a liberation movement while buyers see it as more of a swiss army knife type tool.

Cybersecurity over all.

Cybersecurity and threat detection are seen to be the highest priorities over digital sovereignty and data control. For sovereignty advocates, they should consider reframing the message to being “sovereignty is security.”
